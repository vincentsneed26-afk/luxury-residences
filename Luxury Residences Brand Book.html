03 · Audit & Diagnosis
Where the brand stands today

Two marks. Two voices. Neither is yet authority.

The current identity speaks in two unrelated registers — a fashion-house signature and a tech-startup monogram. The result reads as a property listing, not a private address.

Luxury Residences
Wordmark — current
  • —Generic boutique-script. Reads "spa" or "florist," not real estate.
  • —Illegible below 240px. Fails on signage, app icons, embroidery.
  • —No connection to the LR monogram beside it.
LR
Monogram — current
  • —Geometric chamfer reads tech/SaaS, not residential.
  • —The green dot is unanchored — neither brand colour nor symbol.
  • —No hierarchy: L and R fight for dominance.
Diagnosis

The category is loud. DAMAC sells celebrity. Emaar sells corporate scale. The opening is silence — a brand that behaves like an old European maison transplanted to the Gulf. Identity must move from announcement to hallmark.

I — Strategy · Audit 03 / 20
04 · Brand Story
The narrative

An address is the shortest
autobiography a man writes.

Dubai is a city of declarations. Towers compete; brochures shout; gold finds new surfaces every season. In that climate, the truest luxury is a brand that does not raise its voice.

Luxury Residences exists for the principal who has nothing left to prove. The man whose name opens doors before he reaches them. For him, a home is not a trophy — it is a hallmark, stamped quietly on a life already in motion.

Our work is to design the silence around that hallmark: the paper it is printed on, the threshold it sits above, the single line that introduces him to a city that already knows.

i.
Restraint

Every gesture is the smallest one that still reads as authority.

ii.
Permanence

The mark behaves like an engraving — meant to outlast the season.

iii.
Privacy

The brand never speaks of the resident. Only the resident does.

iv.
Place

Of Dubai, but not loud about it. Geography is given, not gilded.

08 · Logo · Direction I — The Hallmark
Direction I

The Hallmark.

A circular maker's mark — the way a silversmith stamps sterling. Concentric rules carry the name; the centre is held by an italic LR ligature drawn in the manner of a private bookplate.

Why it works
The circle is the oldest authority sign in commerce — coins, seals, sovereign rings. It signals certification, not advertising.
Where it lives
Embossed on letterhead. Etched into brass thresholds. Foil-blocked on a single keycard. Never animated.
Risk
Reads heritage if mishandled — we keep the inner ligature contemporary to balance the centuries.
Strategic fit
Aligns to Sovereign archetype + the Hallmarked pillar. Strongest of the three on authority.
II — Identity · Logo I 08 / 20
09 · Logo · Direction II — The Architecture
Direction II

The Architecture.

A wordmark drawn from architectural drafting traditions — high-contrast Didone capitals, hairline rules above and below, the city carved into the baseline like a foundation stone.

Why it works
The thick-thin contrast of a Didone is the typographic equivalent of a marble column — formal, vertical, classical. It speaks of buildings, not brochures.
Where it lives
Cover of every brochure. Façade signage in cast bronze. Newspaper-format announcements.
Risk
Didones are common in luxury — we earn distinction by cropping the rules tight and pairing with the LR cipher monogram (slide 11).
Strategic fit
Aligns to Permanence + Place. The most "developer-classical" of the three; safe, durable, immediately legible.

Direction III

The Address.

A typographic cipher — L and R divided by a single hairline, sized like the numerals on a townhouse door. The wordmark sits below in Inter at the scale of an engraved nameplate.

Why it works
Modernist restraint. The hairline between the letters is the entire idea — a threshold, a property line, a bookplate divider. Nothing else is decorative.
Where it lives
App icon. Watermark on contracts. Discreet end-card on film. Stitched into bedlinen. The most digital-fluent of the three.
Risk
Reads austere; on its own it can feel cold. Always paired with warm material (cream paper, brass, wood) to soften.
Strategic fit
Aligns to Restraint + Privacy. Strongest on contemporary fluency — the choice if we expect a digital-first audience.

12 · Construction & Clear Space
Geometry

The mark is built on a 10×10 module of side x. The outer ring sits at 5x radius; the inner ring at 4.6x. The LR cap-height is exactly 4x.

Clear space

No graphic, type or edge may enter the dotted frame at 1x on all sides. Print: minimum 32mm / digital: minimum 96px.

Minimum size

Hallmark: 18mm / 72px. Below this size, switch to the LR Cipher.

Surfaces

Foil, blind-emboss, deep-etch, single-pass screen. Never a gradient. Never a drop-shadow. Never on photography without a 30% black scrim.

16 · Imagery
Imagery direction

Dubai, seen from above the noise.

All photography is aerial. The principal sees the city the way the city should be remembered — from the floor where his residence sits. People are absent; geography is the protagonist.

Rules
  1. Aerial only. No interiors of bedrooms, no families at breakfast.
  2. One human silhouette per ten frames at most. Never identifiable.
  3. Process to a 92% saturation, +0.2 contrast curve, gentle warm grade.
  4. Crop to 3:2 or 1:2; never square. The format itself signals architecture.
III — System · Imagery 16 / 20
17 · Grid & Composition
Section iii

A house, written in three lines.

Type sits inside a 12-column field with 32px gutters. Display copy ranges across columns 2–7; supporting copy ranges across 8–11. Column 1 and 12 are reserved for breath.

Composition
  • 12-column grid, 32px gutter, 96px outer margin (print: 24mm).
  • Baseline 8px; all type aligns to it. Caps align to cap-height, not box.
  • Asymmetric layouts by default — the page has weight only on one side. Centred layouts are reserved for ceremonial pages (cover, dedication).
  • Hairlines (0.5px / 0.25mm) divide sections. No filled bars.
  • Page numbers in roman numerals only on ceremonial sections; arabic everywhere else.

A · Concept A — The Script · stationery suite
Client concept · evaluated

Sans-cap "LUXURY" with a script "Residencies."

A type-only lockup that pairs widely-tracked geometric capitals with a flowing handwritten companion — the script reads as a maker's signature underneath the formal name.

Direction notes

The script flourishes warmly but loses authority below 18mm. We recommend reserving this lockup for printed correspondence and packaging, paired with a simpler cap-only variant for signage and digital where the script will pixel-clog at small sizes.

II — Identity · Concept A A / C
B · Concept B — The Bracket · environmental suite
Client concept · evaluated

Architectural bracket monogram, built like a frame.

A linework "LR" enclosed in a rectangular bracket that reads as a doorway, a property line, a frame around an address. The strongest of the three for environmental and architectural applications.

Direction notes

The bracket is structurally legible at any scale — from a 6mm app icon to a 6m façade letter. The negative-space "R" rewards proximity. Recommended as the architectural mark; pair with a Cormorant wordmark on stationery to soften.

II — Identity · Concept B B / C
C · Concept C — The Block · publication suite
Client concept · evaluated

A solid block monogram, set beside the wordmark.

An emphatic geometric LR drawn from a single closed silhouette — high contrast, immediate impact. Best deployed on covers and editorial moments where presence matters more than refinement.

Direction notes

The block monogram is the loudest of the three concepts — closer to fashion-house posture than to Pentagram's recommended hallmark. Use sparingly: covers, season-launch posters, end-card moments. Always paired with extended tracking on the wordmark to keep the composition aristocratic, not athletic.
